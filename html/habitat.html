Habitat and Population

The Surculus Fabam is found in the savannas of central Africa. The savanna is a very challenging; environment because even though it is hot all year round, there is very little rainfall (“Savanna Environment''). The very; dry climate resulted in the Surculus Fabam developing long roots to absorb all the water in the soil. In nature, there is no fairness and it is all about survival of the fittest. Meaning; that the Surculus Fabam’s long roots adapted to attack the roots of nearby planets to take their water supply. Plants located; in the Central African savanna include thorn bushes, acacia Senegal, candelabra tree, jackal berry tree, umbrella thorn acacia, whistling thorn, Bermuda grass, baobabs, and elephant grass (“African Savanna Biome”). The populations of these plants have exponentially decreased as the Surculus Fabam increased due to this new gain in nutrients. The Savanna also commonly experiences fires due to the dry environment; however, species such as the Surculus Fabam and different kinds of grasses have gone through generations of Natural selection to build up stems strong enough to withstand blazing temperatures (“Savanna Environment''). There are a large number of herbivores in the Savanna due to all the vegetation such as giraffes and zebras. This is the reason why the Surculus Fabam mutated to develop spikier peas to not be the main course for those animals.

The population of Surculus Fabam has drastically increased due to less competition from nearby plants. Similar; to defensive plants such as the Venus Flytrap, the Surculus Fabam is at the bottom of the food web. This is because despite the defensive pea shooting mechanisms, at the end of the day, this is a plant and plants will and do get eaten by herbivores such as elephants and zebras. However, the Surculus Fabam continues to play a crucial role in the circle of life because after the animals that eat it die, the nutrients return to the soil, which is then reabsorbed by the plant. In addition, the defensive adaptations are enough to maintain a high population of other plants in the ecosystem.
